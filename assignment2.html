From drop to redistribution: how COVID-19 reshaped crime patterns in San Francisco

When COVID-19 hit in 2020, daily life in cities changed dramatically. Mobility dropped, businesses closed, and public spaces emptied. A natural question follows: what happened to crime?

At first glance, one might expect crime to simply decrease. But crime is not one single phenomenon. Different crime categories depend on different opportunities, routines, and forms of public activity. This raises a deeper question: did COVID reduce crime, or did it reshape when, where, and what kind of crime was recorded?

In this data story, I use San Francisco police incident data to study how crime evolved before, during, and after the pandemic. I focus on three categories — theft, vehicle theft, and drug offense — and analyze them across three dimensions: long-term trends, geographic concentration, and daily timing patterns.

The overall picture is that COVID-19 did not simply lower crime. Instead, it produced a structural break: some crimes fell sharply, some became more visible afterward, and daily patterns shifted. Together, these changes suggest that the pandemic reshaped the structure of crime rather than affecting all crimes in the same way.

1. A structural break: crime did not evolve uniformly

Figure 1. Yearly recorded incidents for theft, vehicle theft, and drug offense. Theft drops sharply in 2020, while vehicle theft rises afterward, highlighting diverging responses across crime categories.

The first figure reveals a clear structural break around 2020. Theft, which had been rising before the pandemic, drops sharply at the onset of COVID-19. Vehicle theft behaves very differently, increasing in the following years, while drug offenses continue a longer downward trend.

This divergence matters. If the pandemic had simply reduced all crime, we would expect all curves to move in roughly the same direction. Instead, the figure suggests that COVID-19 redistributed crime across categories. Changes in mobility, public activity, commuting, and economic conditions likely affected different crime types in different ways.

2. Crime did not spread evenly across the city

Figure 2. Geographic distribution of theft across San Francisco police districts. A small number of districts account for a large share of incidents.

The spatial distribution of theft is clearly uneven. A relatively small number of districts account for a much larger share of recorded incidents, while other parts of the city remain far less affected. This suggests that theft is tied to specific urban environments rather than distributed uniformly across San Francisco.

This is important for the overall story. The pandemic changed total crime levels, but crime still remained concentrated in already active areas. In other words, COVID appears to have changed volume more than geography: the city did not become spatially uniform, and central districts continued to concentrate a large share of theft incidents.

3. Crime follows daily routines — and these shifted during COVID

Figure 3. Hourly crime patterns before, during, and after COVID. Crime follows a daily rhythm, with peaks later in the day and noticeable shifts during the pandemic period.

The final figure adds the daily time dimension. Across all three categories, crime follows a clear rhythm: incidents are relatively rare in the early morning, then rise from midday into the afternoon and evening. This shows that crime is closely tied to ordinary daily routines and patterns of urban activity.

During the COVID period, however, these patterns shift. Some categories display sharper peaks or different timing than before, which is consistent with the disruption of commuting, work schedules, nightlife, and street activity during the pandemic. This suggests that COVID-19 changed not only how much crime was recorded, but also when it was most likely to occur.

Conclusion

Overall, the pandemic period looks like a turning point in San Francisco crime patterns. Rather than simply reducing crime, COVID-19 reshaped it across several dimensions. We observe a break in long-term trends, continued concentration in a few districts, and noticeable changes in daily timing patterns.

These results also show why it is misleading to speak of “crime” as if it were one single thing. Different categories respond differently to disruptions in mobility, routine activity, and economic life. COVID-19 did not affect all crimes uniformly: it changed their relative importance, their timing, and their visibility in the city.

At the same time, police incident data captures recorded incidents, not all crime in some pure sense. Reporting behavior, police presence, and enforcement practices also shape the data. That matters especially when interpreting a major disruption like COVID-19.

In short, the pandemic did not make crime disappear. It changed where, when, and how it appeared in San Francisco.
