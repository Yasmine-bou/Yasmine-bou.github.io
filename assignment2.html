How COVID-19 changed crime in San Francisco

This short data story explores how crime patterns in San Francisco changed before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic. Using police incident data, I focus on three crime categories — theft, vehicle theft, and drug offense — and look at how they evolved over time, where they were concentrated, and when they happened during the day.

The main idea is that COVID did not simply reduce crime. Instead, it seems to have changed its shape: some categories dropped sharply, others became more visible after 2020, and daily timing patterns also shifted. At the same time, this dataset reflects recorded incidents, not all crime, so reporting and policing practices must also be kept in mind.

1. A clear break around 2020

Figure 1. Yearly recorded incidents for theft, vehicle theft, and drug offense. Theft drops sharply in 2020, while vehicle theft rises afterward. This suggests that the pandemic period changed crime patterns rather than simply lowering all crime equally.

The first figure shows a visible structural break around 2020. Theft, which had been rising before the pandemic, falls sharply at the onset of COVID. Vehicle theft behaves differently, increasing in the following years, while drug offense continues a longer downward trend. This already suggests that the pandemic did not affect all crime types in the same way.

2. Theft is concentrated in a few districts

Figure 2. Geographic distribution of theft across San Francisco police districts. Theft is not evenly distributed: a small number of central districts account for a much larger share of recorded incidents.

The spatial picture is also uneven. Theft is concentrated in a limited set of districts rather than spread uniformly across the city. This makes the pandemic story more complex: changes in crime are not only about totals over time, but also about where incidents remain concentrated.

3. Crime also has a daily rhythm

Figure 3. Hourly crime patterns before, during, and after COVID. Incidents tend to be less frequent in the early morning and more common from midday into the evening. Differences across periods suggest that the pandemic also changed when incidents were recorded.

The final figure adds the time-of-day dimension. All three crime categories follow a clear daily rhythm, with lower levels in the early morning and higher levels later in the day. The overall shapes remain similar, but some shifts appear between the pre-COVID, COVID, and post-COVID periods, which suggests that the pandemic affected not only how much crime was recorded, but also when it appeared.

Conclusion

Overall, the pandemic period looks like a turning point in San Francisco crime data. It coincides with a sharp change in yearly totals, continued geographic concentration, and noticeable differences in daily timing patterns.

Still, these results should be interpreted carefully. Police incident data measures recorded incidents, not crime in some pure sense. Changes in mobility, reporting, and policing can all shape what appears in the dataset. That is especially important when discussing crime around a major disruption like COVID-19.
